The UK government is running a [consultation](https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/growing-up-in-the-online-world-a-national-consultation) on _"major measures to protect children on social media, gaming platforms and AI chatbots"_.

I have [strong opinions](/blog/age-verification-is-a-scam/) about Age Verification technology and the rollout of it across the winder Internet, particularly about the impacts on privacy and security for users. 

The prevailing direction of political thinking is to use this technology to limit access to social media sites for *everyone*. Social media sites (which we must presume will include forums, federated sites like Mastodon, chat applications like Discord, etc) would be _required_ to age-verify every single UK user - effectively ending the ability for users to be anonymous or partially-anonymous to online platforms.

I took the time to express my opinions in this consultation, and I encourage you to do the same. There are certainly further points I wish I'd made but was not given the opportunity to do so, but I think my responses cover my general attitude to these proposals.

I've included my responses below. Note that I didn't respond to every single question on the consultation because there were some that I did not feel I was equipped to answer or that I didn't have a sufficiently strong opinion on. 

My responses are encoded as quotes. All other text is from the [consultation document](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/69a494a6286b6fdc85daeb1c/growing_up_in_the_online_world-national-conversation.pdf) which has been made available under the [Open Government License v3.0](https://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/doc/open-government-licence/version/3/).

## The Consultation

**What are the benefits of social media use, and being online, for children?**

> Children are people. They have unique interests, hobbies, fascinations, problems, anxieties, challenges. Their experiences shape them, as all of our experiences have shaped us. 
> 
> "Social Media" isn't Facebook or Twitter, as much as the media would love to frame it as such. 
> 
> It's Forums. It's federated, interest-specific online social spaces. It's gaming groups. It's support subreddits for LGBTQ people. It's "vent" channels on Discord where personal problems can be discussed with impartial friends instead of judging teachers and family.
> 
> Taking that away from young people would be devastating.

**What are the harms or risks of social media use, and being online, for children?**

> A lack of context. That's when social media can become dangerous. Without context, provided exclusively by parents and guardians, social media can appear overwhelming and all-consuming. It can start to define us rather than be an expression of ourselves. This is true of any kind of media, though. Social media is just the lowest-friction route, and is therefore the contemporary example.

**Do you think the benefits of children using social media, and being online, outweigh the risks, or the other way around?**

> Benefits somewhat outweigh the risks.

**Would you support a legal requirement for social media services to have a minimum age of access?**

> No.

**Please explain the reasoning behind your answers about minimum age requirements:**

> Not only would this leave millions of young people seriously isolated from the world, their distant friends overseas and from support for their issues and problems; but it would introduce a requirement for all *adults* to submit incredibly sensitive identity information to inherently untrustworthy services that profit from data collection.
> 
> The Online Safety Act introduced similar requirements for adult content and user-to-user services, and we are already seeing the disastrous consequences of that. Already, Discord, an online chat service, has suffered a data breach leading to the leaking of 70,000 identity documents that were only required to satisfy age verification requirements.
> 
> The companies providing Age Verification services will profit greatly from the data collection requirements imposed by this. Many are owned by untrustworthy U.S. companies linked to surveillance and military applications (Palantir, for instance).
> 
> This decision would punish already-struggling, isolated young people; while also severely compromising the privacy and safety of every single adult.
>
> I urge the government to consider mechanisms that support and empower parents in managing their children's access to social media rather than schemes and legislation that put millions of adults at risk.

**What do you think the impacts would be of having a minimum age requirement higher than 13 for social media services?**

> Young people would effectively lose the ability to have friendships online. Cut off from the world, isolated from existing connections. After the loss of mobility caused by Brexit, this would be another huge social and cultural loss for young people. We risk creating an isolated generation of children. It's not healthy, and parents/carers will also suffer the impacts of that.
> 
> It would become effectively impossible to use user-to-user sites anonymously or with any degree of privacy. That is absolutely dangerous. Journalists, security researchers, online personalities, or just people who choose to retain their privacy online would all be negatively impacted.
> 
> Innovative, small federated social media services (see: Mastodon, GoToSocial) would effectively be extinguished by this requirement, replaced by platforms offered by the giant companies like X (née Twitter) and Meta (née Facebook).

**At what age do you think the age of digital consent in the UK should be set for information society services?**

Under UK GDPR, ‘information society services’ often need to seek consent to use people’s data for activities such as profiling for advertising purposes. **Information society services are most online services that are provided for commercial purposes**, including websites, apps, online gaming, social media, search engines, online marketplaces, etc. Children can only give their consent for their data to be used for such purposes if they are 13 or above. For children under this age, services are required to make reasonable efforts to verify consent from those with parental responsibility.

> Other. I really don't think it matters what you set this at. See below.

**What risks or burdens may be associated with raising the minimum age of digital consent?**

> Parents/guardians should be the gatekeepers in this system and should hold the responsibility/agency for this. 
> 
> Any `are you > x years old?` age checks will be bypassed or spoofed. Attempting to improve the rigour of these will violate the privacy of all users by collecting huge amounts of personally identifiable information.
> 
> Not every site has a multi-million pound budget to implement this sort of mechanism either. Remember that many social media sites are operated by volunteers and have a small number of users.

**What should be considered to make raising the digital age of consent effective and workable?**

> Legislate to mandate the provision of tools to help parents implement these limits. Perhaps if sites could provide a "Recommended Age" header and parents could set up their computer/devices to respect these.
> 
> Any implementation must NOT impact the privacy or security of users in any way.

**What are the benefits to children of using AI chatbots?**

An AI chatbot is an artificial intelligence system that can replicate human-like conversations with users. They can generate images, videos, text or audio in response to ‘prompts’ from users in real time. They may also remember context and personalise responses to individual users. These services can draw from a variety of datasets including live search results and large language models. AI chatbots can be standalone services or embedded within another service. Examples of this include ChatGPT (standalone) or embedded within services, such as Grok (within X).

> AI chatbots offer few, if any, benefits when compared to other resources and tools online. They can be useful for searching as a type of fuzzy-match search algorithm as long as results are verified carefully.
> 
> I think it is critically important that children understand what these systems are. I do not subscribe to the belief that they should be restricted from accessing chatbots, since I do not think that serves any useful purpose.

### Age assurance

> If online minimum age restrictions were to be introduced, services in scope would need to prove that their users were above the required age. Age assurance methods include, but are not limited to, facial age estimation, photo ID matching, AI and biometric based systems, and age inference (where AI predicts age based on online behaviour).

**To what extent do you agree or disagree with the following statement: **

_“Adults should complete age checks more often, if it means children are safer online”_

> Strongly disagree.

**What should be considered to make minimum age restrictions effective and workable?**

> They will never be effective or workable.
> 
> Age Verification lobbyists and vendors have presented an idea to governments that these tools will solve societal problems. They will not. The reality of these tools is that they trade privacy and freedom for the idea of online safety.
> 
> The reality is that they will be bypassed and spoofed, while users that cooperate will suffer the inevitable data breaches and privacy loss.

**What do you think the impacts might be from requiring age assurance across a greater number of online platforms?**

> There will be massive data breaches that will leave millions of UK citizens vulnerable to identity theft, life-ruining privacy breaches and worse.
> 
> The companies that provide these services are unregulated and exist simply to turn a profit. Even the "good" ones will eventually be eroded. They will be bought by Private Equity firms, standards will be dropped, breaches will happen, and users will pay the price.

**How, if at all, could age assurance be made more effective?**

> By not being implemented or enforced.

**What should be considered when assessing the effectiveness of age-verification and age-assurance technologies?**

> Their impact on privacy and security of users. Even client-side facial age estimation, photo ID matching, AI and biometric based systems are vulnerable to software supply chain attacks. 
> 
> There is no safe way to implement this. Consider other options that empower parents, guardians and carers before buying into for-profit technology solutions.

### VPNs

Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) are tools that create a secure private connection between your device and the internet. They are used for several purposes, such as protecting sensitive communications and protecting privacy, including in a corporate context.

**What methods to circumvent online safety rules do you think children in the UK use, beyond Virtual Private Networks (VPNs), or similar technologies?**

> There are thousands of ways to avoid and bypass censorship online. The UK government even suggests some of these methods in official advice to citizens visiting certain countries with heavily Internet surveillance or censorship.
> 
> This is "whack a mole". Every whack will hurt our privacy, safety and security, while doing nothing to improve the safety of children.
> 
> As I have expressed in all other answers, only improved education, tools, and parental/guardian guidance can improve the safety of children online.

**Which of the options below do you think the government should prioritise to reduce circumvention of online safety rules in the UK?**

* More education for children ✅
* Restricting children's access to VPNs
* None of the above
* Don't know/Prefer not to say

**To what extent do you agree or disagree with the following statement:**

_“Everyone should go through age checks to access a VPN if it would prevent children using them”_

> Strongly disagree.

**What do you think the impacts would be if VPNs were age-restricted?**

> This is perhaps one of the worst possible proposals I have seen in this debate.
> 
> Firstly, it would place the UK on the same level of Internet control as China and Russia. Please consider the impact to whistleblowing, journalism, security research, and more if it becomes essentially impossible to anonymise internet usage in the UK.
> 
> Commercial/consumer VPNs exist as a privacy tool. Submitting identity information to them defeats their purpose.
> 
> There are also thousands of other uses for VPNs in the IT sector and beyond. Most users working from home are using a VPN right now. Connecting to and managing remote computer systems usually relies on VPN connections. IoT (Internet of Things) devices connect to their backend services via VPNs. A VPN can be configured and launched on cloud computing resource (Amazon EC2) or as a service (Amazon AWS Site to Site VPN) in 5 minutes. A VPN can be run using free & open source software on a friend's computer overseas. Platforms exist that anonymise and route Internet traffic specifically for the purpose of censorship evasion without a specific endpoint being configured.
> 
> In summary, the impact of this would be huge, but it would not solve any problems at all. Once again, it is the wrong solution.

**What should be considered to make age-restricting VPNs effective and workable?**

> It cannot be made effective or workable. It is a path to curtailed freedom of expression and online censorship and should not be pursued.
